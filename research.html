Tumor Microenvironment in Breast Cancer Progression and Therapeutic Resistance

Regulation and Function of ECM Remodeling in Triple-Negative Breast Cancer (TNBC) Progression and Drug Resistance

Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is an aggressive subtype with poor prognosis, disproportionately affecting young African-American women. TNBC lacks responsiveness to hormone therapy and HER2-targeted treatments, making it imperative to uncover the mechanisms driving its progression and to identify actionable therapeutic targets. Our research has identified a transcriptional network governing extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling and several key regulatory hubs that are significantly upregulated in human TNBC (Cancer Res, 2015; Nat Commun, 2018; PNAS, 2023). We have also uncovered novel crosstalk between ECM remodeling, epigenetic regulation, and metabolic reprogramming during breast cancer progression (PNAS, 2023). These findings have significantly advanced our understanding of tumor–microenvironment interactions and their role in cancer cell plasticity. Using genetically engineered mouse models, patient-derived xenografts, and tumor organoids, we are currently evaluating whether targeting these ECM regulatory hubs can suppress TNBC progression and enhance the efficacy of chemotherapy and immunotherapy.

Intercellular Communication in Breast Cancer Progression and Metastasis

Interactions between cancer cells and stromal components within the tumor microenvironment (TME) are critical drivers of breast cancer progression and metastasis. Our current work focuses on how intercellular communication—mediated by ECM components, metabolites, and extracellular RNAs—promotes obesity-associated breast cancer progression, metastasis, and immune evasion. These projects aim to define novel mechanisms of tumor-stroma interaction and uncover potential therapeutic strategies to disrupt these signaling networks.
